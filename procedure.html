What is the ABC Model?

The ABC in ABC model stands for Adversity, Beliefs and Consequences.

The ABC model describes how the beliefs we have about an adversity in a situation largely determine the consequences for us.

Whenever we experience an emotion in a situation, it is because we are telling ourselves something about the situation. These beliefs can take some time and effort for us to uncover them.

The ABC model helps us clarify the thoughts we have in a situation that lead us to:

  • form an unhelpful negative emotion
  • subsequently engage in unhelpful behaviours
    • Unhelpfulness here is defined as anything that is not in your best interests, harms you or your relationships with others.
  • generate unhelpful thoughts that further build on the initial thought we had.

1. Define the Problem

1.1 Identify a specific instance of a problem

The first step is to decide what problem you want to work on.

You want to be as specific as possible. Even if a problem has occurred many times, it helps to pinpoint a specific instance so you can be clear about what you were thinking and feeling at the time.

We call this specific instance the situation. We define it as the objective account of what is going on -- it is what a group of impartial observers would agree has happened.

{% call c.box() %}

Example:

Problem: I'm not getting along with my partner

Unclear: They criticise me all the time

Situation: They criticised me last Tuesday when I tried to help with the dishes

{% endcall %}

1.2 Determine what you can control or influence

Before looking at the ABC model, consider your options:

{% call c.box() %}

Direct control -- is there a concrete action you can take about the problem? eg. starting a task you are procrastinating on

Indirect influence -- can you influence the situation to lessen the impact of the problem, eg. explaining to a loved one why their comments are upsetting

{% endcall %}

If you have done everything possible or control or influence the problem and you still struggle with unhelpful emotions or behaviours, then the last step available is to cope with the situation. This is where the ABC model becomes relevant.

Learning to cope more effectively with unhelpful emotions is helpful whenever you have a problem as it would be more difficult to control or influence a situation when your emotions are hindering you from taking action.

2. Define the Adversity

If the situation is the objective account of what happened, then the adversity is the thought that comes to our mind regarding the situation. It is the initial, knee-jerk answer to the question “What does this situation mean for me, others, or the world around me?”

The adversity is usually a subjective opinion, but it can also be an objective fact about what happened.

{% call c.box() %}

Example:

Situation: I lost my job.

Adversity: I don’t know how I can pay next fortnight’s bills

{% endcall %}

There are usually many possible adversities generated from a situation, so using the same example:

{% call c.box() %}

I didn’t try hard enough at work to keep my job

I lost the job because my boss doesn’t like me

My wife is going to be disappointed in me

I won't be able to sleep because of this

{% endcall %}

Different adversities generate different types of unhelpful emotions. It is possible to have multiple unhelpful negative emotions about a single situation.

Another way of figuring out the adversity to ask yourself what you would want to remove from the situation to make the unhelpful negative emotion go away.

3.3 Identify secondary emotions

When we experience an unhelpful negative emotion, we might also have an emotional response to the initial emotion. These are known as secondary emotions.

In practice, this means we can be depressed about getting angry, shameful about being depressed, anxious about being jealous, and so on.

{% call c.box() %}

Example of problematic worrying:

Situation: You are anxious

Adversity: Anxiety means something bad will happen

Belief: I must not be anxious otherwise I couldn't stand it

Consequence: Anxiety continues to worsen

{% endcall %}

The initial, primary emotion becomes a new situation and corresponding adversity which activates another unhelpful belief that leads to the unhelpful negative emotion.

Consider which emotion is the most problematic for you. Addressing the secondary emotion might be more helpful if it liberates you to focus on the primary emotion more effectively.

3.4 Set a goal for the problem

If you are experiencing an adversity, you are likely to experience some form of negative emotion, so setting a goal of having positive or no negative emotion would be unrealistic and unhelpful.

Behavioral goals are the most helpful -- ie. what you would be doing differently if you were coping more effectively with the problem.

4. Identify Unhelpful Beliefs

Unhelpful beliefs are unhelpful because they are rigid and extreme.

When we have rigid and extreme beliefs, we are insisting:

  • that our expectations must be met regardless of what the reality is (rigid)
  • and if the expectation is not met, we would suffer an insurmountable negative outcome (extreme)

See {{c.link('beliefs', "Beliefs")}} for more information on rigid and extreme beliefs.

4.1 Identifying the demand

To identify the rigid belief, or the demand, we can ask:

What am I insisting should happen for myself, others, or the situation?

You might give another adversity as an answer to that.

Note: use of the word "should" does not necessarily indicate a problematic demand. Shoulds can be used to express:

  • Preference: I should put less sugar in the dough so the cake is not too sweet for me
  • Fact: For a fire to start, there should be a source of fuel

Shoulds become unhelpful when they express an absolute need for something to happen.

4.2 Identifying the evaluation

When we know what we are demanding in an unhelpful belief, we can then work out what we are expecting will happen if the demand is not met. This is the evaluation.

We want to keep asking ourselves what is the result of an unmet demand until we reach a point where we express a global negative sentiment about one or more of the following:

  1. Self
  2. Others
  3. The situation, the world, or the future

It might take some time to identify the unhelpful beliefs, and arriving at the evaluation might require asking the question repeatedly. For example:

{% call c.box() %}

I might get fined for not paying a bill.

What would then happen?

I might have less money in my account.

What's the consequence of that?

I might not be able to afford something later.

What's so bad about that?

Then I can't have what I need.

And if you can't have what you need, what then?

It would be awful and I wouldn't be able to cope without it.

{% endcall %}

Talking to another person (a trusted friend, a qualified counsellor) can help a lot with figuring out what unhelpful beliefs are being activated in the situation.

5. Dispute Unhelpful Beliefs

There are several questions you can use to dispute beliefs:

1. Is it realistic?

Quite often we can hold unrealistic beliefs without even being aware of them. A common one is a belief that we must be approved or liked by everybody.

2. Is it helping me?

3. Is it logical?

4. Is it in line with my values?

5. Would I teach this belief to someone I care about?
